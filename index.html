Our Mission

Justice Through Clemency

CAN-DO Foundation educates the public about the collateral damage of the conspiracy law and advocates for people serving extreme sentences for nonviolent offenses.

Until current drug laws change, there will be a steady stream of people serving decades behind bars. An educated public is the first step toward real justice reform.

Full Presidential Pardon

Amy Povah — President Trump granted a full pardon to Amy Povah, the founder of the CAN-DO (Clemency for All Non-violent Drug Offenders) Foundation. In the 1990s, Ms. Povah served 9 years of a 24 year sentence for a drug offense before President Clinton commuted her remaining prison sentence in 2000.

Since her release, she has become a voice for the incarcerated, a champion for criminal justice reform, and was a strong advocate for the passage of the First Step Act. Those assisted by Ms. Povah's organization include Ms. Adrianne Miller, whose remaining prison sentence the President commuted.

Chapter One

The Woman Before the Sentence

Amy Ralston Povah was 28 years old, a successful Vice President of Sales at Commonwealth Bancorp — a legitimate second-mortgage lending company owned by her then-husband, Charles "Sandy" Pofahl. She had consistently closed the highest percentage of loans during the company's first three months. She had no criminal record, no drug history, and no knowledge that Sandy was secretly manufacturing MDMA (Ecstasy) overseas.

In 1989, federal agents raided Amy's Los Angeles home. They gave her an ultimatum: wear a wire and infiltrate Sandy's organization, or face a conspiracy indictment carrying 20 years to life. Amy refused — she genuinely didn't know enough to cooperate. The agents promised to "destroy her life." They kept that promise.

Two years of harassment followed. Then the indictment came.

Case Facts: U.S. v. Amy Ralston Pofahl

Court: U.S. District Court, Waco, TX
Judge: Walter S. Smith Jr.
Charge: Conspiracy to manufacture & distribute MDMA
Drug Quantity: 3+ million MDMA tablets (Sandy's entire operation)
Amy's Actual Role: Collected bail money for husband after his arrest
Sentence: 24 years, 4 months
Sandy Pofahl's Sentence: 4 yrs Germany + 3 yrs U.S. probation
Co-Defendant Affidavits: 6 swore Amy was not involved
Congressional Support: 17 House & Senate members wrote for clemency
The Case

U.S. v. Amy Ralston Pofahl

Amy was flown to Waco, Texas, denied bail, and held in the McLennan County Jail for an entire year awaiting trial. Her court-appointed attorney's only interest was pushing her toward a guilty plea.

The majority of the evidence at trial pertained to her estranged husband, but the jury focused on Amy in his absence. Sandy was in a German prison but was never brought to the U.S. to testify — because his testimony would have destroyed the prosecution's case. They had portrayed Amy as involved from the conspiracy's inception.

The only people who testified against Amy were co-defendants who offered false testimony in exchange for reduced sentences. Both are free today.

Sandy Pofahl — the kingpin who manufactured millions of MDMA tablets — received 4 years in Germany and 3 years U.S. probation. Amy received 24 years, 4 months for collecting bail money at his request.

For her final appeal, Amy collected six affidavits from co-defendants who each swore that they did not believe Amy was involved in the conspiracy for which she was charged. Even Sandy Pofahl asserted that she had not been involved until after he'd been arrested.

These affidavits completely refuted the government's misleading charge to the jury that Amy had been involved from the conspiracy's inception. The Court denied Amy's valid appeal without so much as an explanation.

"Women are far likelier than men to be serving long sentences because of the drugs a spouse or partner sold, due to broad application of the conspiracy law — but the converse is not true. Few men, if any, ended up with 20 years to life based on illicit activity attributed to a wife or girlfriend." — Amy Povah

July 7, 2000

From Conspiracy to Clemency

From her cell at FCI Dublin, Amy never stopped fighting. She launched a massive letter-writing campaign joined by friends, loved ones, and activists inspired by her story.

In 1999, Glamour Magazine published a six-page article by Oscar-nominated journalist David France that shocked the nation — the first major publication to expose how the conspiracy law was being used to sentence wives and girlfriends of drug dealers as if they were kingpins.

Court TV followed with the documentary "Prisoners of Love." Scripps Howard, Canadian Broadcasting, and the Sun Valley Times covered her case. Seventeen members of the House and Senate wrote letters supporting Amy's clemency.

The spirit of justice prevailed. On July 7, 2000, President Clinton commuted Amy's sentence. She burst through the gates of FCI Dublin — free after 9 years and 3 months.

"Glamour Magazine was a game changer because it enabled key politicians to understand what happened in my case and as a result, they were not afraid to support my clemency." — Amy Povah

Post-release, Amy appeared on the Montel Williams Show. A second Glamour article — "Free At Last" by Stephanie Dolgoff — documented her extraordinary campaign and fairy-tale ending to a long nightmare.

While still incarcerated, Amy wrote her prison roommate Lau Ching Chin's clemency petition. Lau had received 17 years for passing a single phone message to a boyfriend who was involved in drug dealing — she had no idea what the message was about. Clinton granted Lau's clemency before leaving office. CAN-DO was already being born.

The Foundation

Building CAN-DO

Amy's freedom was bittersweet. She remained in contact with women she served time with — many with cases more tragic than her own. Because Amy knew how easily she could still be behind bars, she vowed to help others who needed a voice on the outside. CAN-DO — Clemency for All Nonviolent Drug Offenders — became her life's mission, obtaining 501(c)(3) status in 2004.

⚖️ Clemency Advocacy

Profiles cases, files petitions, builds coalitions of support. 44 CAN-DO women received Obama clemency out of 105 women total. Continues advocating across administrations — Clinton, Obama, Trump, Biden.

📚 Conspiracy Law Education

Amy is recognized as one of the nation's leading experts on how the conspiracy statute ensnares low-level and peripheral defendants, especially women.

📋 Top 25 Lists

Curates and publicizes the Top 25 Men and Women Deserving Clemency — putting names, faces, and stories before decision-makers and the public.

🐻 Teddy Bear-Care Program

Helps incarcerated parents maintain bonds with their children through comfort items and connection.

🎬 Harm Reduction Productions

In 2010 Amy founded her own film production company. Featured in "Incarcerating US," "Commuted," and produced "420: The Documentary" and "This Is Not Life."

🏛️ First Step Act Champion

Was a strong advocate for the passage of the First Step Act — landmark bipartisan legislation reforming federal sentencing and expanding early-release programs.

2014–2018

White House Vigils & the Obama Era

Amy conducted four CAN-DO vigils in front of the White House and helped organize a fifth with #Cut50 and Change.org in the final days of the Obama administration. Amy has been to the White House on three occasions for issues related to clemency and criminal justice reform.

Of the 105 women who received clemency under President Obama, 44 were CAN-DO members that Amy and her team advocated for. Out of Obama's 1,715 total commutations, only 105 went to women — and CAN-DO was behind nearly half of them.

In 2018, CAN-DO recommended Alice Marie Johnson for clemency. After Kim Kardashian presented the case to President Trump, Johnson's life sentence was commuted. The administration subsequently asked CAN-DO for a list of similar cases — Amy provided a list of 17 women and 6 men.

January 20, 2021

Full Presidential Pardon

Amy Povah — President Trump granted a full pardon to Amy Povah, the founder of the CAN-DO (Clemency for All Non-violent Drug Offenders) Foundation. In the 1990s, Ms. Povah served 9 years of a 24 year sentence for a drug offense before President Clinton commuted her remaining prison sentence in 2000.

Since her release, she has become a voice for the incarcerated, a champion for criminal justice reform, and was a strong advocate for the passage of the First Step Act. Those assisted by Ms. Povah's organization include Ms. Adrianne Miller, whose remaining prison sentence the President commuted.

Read the full White House statement →

The full pardon was the highest form of executive clemency — going beyond the commutation she received from Clinton. Amy's advocacy had come full circle: from receiving mercy to securing it for others, across multiple administrations and both parties.

2021–2024

Michael Montalvo — Free After 37 Years

It was one of the greatest days of Amy's life when she picked up Michael Montalvo from FCI Phoenix after he served 37 years on a LIFE sentence. Amy had been communicating with Michael dating back to 1994 — she advocated for him for three decades until his clemency was finally granted by President Biden.

Michael was a 78-year-old grandfather, a first-time offender sentenced to mandatory life without parole for a 1980s non-violent drug conspiracy. He maintained three decades without any disciplinary infraction — a model prisoner. After declining a 15 and 10 year offer to plead guilty, he was sentenced to life as a "trial penalty" for exercising his Sixth Amendment right to a jury trial.

March 2025

Testimony Before the U.S. Sentencing Commission

In March 2025, Amy testified before the U.S. Sentencing Commission during public hearings on proposed amendments to the federal sentencing guidelines. Drawing on decades of frontline advocacy and her own lived experience, she addressed the disproportionate impact of conspiracy law on peripheral defendants — particularly women ensnared by the actions of partners.

This was a milestone: the woman who once faced a 24-year sentence was now a recognized expert helping shape the very sentencing framework that had been used against her and thousands of others.
