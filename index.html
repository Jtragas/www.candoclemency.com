The Organization

CAN-DO Is the Main Story

CAN-DO (Clemency for All Nonviolent Drug Offenders) is a national advocacy foundation focused on people serving excessive sentences for non-violent federal offenses. We build clemency and release pathways while helping families navigate every stage of the process.

This site is now structured around current impact first — videos, releases, and active reform work — while Amy's personal story lives in the About section as important background context.

From direct case support to public testimony, CAN-DO's work centers the people still inside and the families waiting at home.

About Amy

Founder Background (Supporting Context)

Amy’s personal case history remains important context for CAN-DO’s mission. This section keeps her story available without making her the homepage focal point.

Read Amy's full story on the About page →

July 7, 2000

Clemency: President Clinton

From prison, Amy attracted Glamour Magazine, Court TV, Scripps Howard, and Canadian Broadcasting. The Glamour article by Oscar-nominated journalist David France was a game-changer — exposing how the conspiracy law sentences wives and girlfriends as kingpins.

The follow-up documentary Prisoners of Love on Court TV amplified the story nationally. Post-release, Amy appeared on the Montel Williams Show and Glamour published a second article, "Free At Last" by Stephanie Dolgoff. Read the article →

"Glamour Magazine was a game changer because it enabled key politicians to understand what happened in my case, and they were not afraid to support my clemency."

Read about the day Amy met President Clinton →

▲ Compilation from Glamour, Court TV, and "Incarcerating US" — the full story from indictment to freedom.

January 20, 2021

Full Presidential Pardon

On his final day in office, President Trump granted Amy a full pardon — going beyond the Clinton commutation. The White House recognized her as "a voice for the incarcerated, a champion for criminal justice reform, and a strong advocate for the First Step Act."

The statement specifically cited Adrianne Miller — whose remaining sentence Trump commuted that same day — as someone assisted by Amy's organization. Her advocacy came full circle: from receiving mercy to securing it for others, across administrations and both parties.

Read the official White House statement →

March 12-13, 2025

U.S. Sentencing Commission Testimony

In March 2025, Amy testified before the U.S. Sentencing Commission during public hearings on proposed federal sentencing guidelines amendments — addressing conspiracy law's disproportionate impact on peripheral defendants, particularly women ensnared by partners' actions.

A milestone: the woman who once faced a 24-year sentence is now a recognized expert helping shape the very sentencing framework once used against her and thousands of others.

Complete Timeline

1989
Federal Raid
DEA agents raided Amy's LA home, demanding she cooperate against her husband or face 20-to-life. She refused.
1991
Sentenced: 24 Years, 4 Months
Held responsible for 3+ million MDMA tablets her husband manufactured. Sandy received just 3 years probation.

Glamour Magazine Exposé
Oscar-nominated journalist David France published a 6-page feature — first major publication to expose how conspiracy law ensnares women.
2000
Clemency — President Clinton
On July 7th, after 17 members of Congress wrote in support, President Clinton commuted Amy's sentence. Free after 9 years, 3 months.
2000
Lau Ching Chin Freed
Amy wrote her prison roommate's clemency petition. Lau received 17 years for passing a single phone message. Clinton granted her clemency.

CAN-DO Foundation — 501(c)(3)
Clemency for All Nonviolent Drug Offenders officially established.
2010
Harm Reduction Productions
Founded film production company to amplify stories of injustice through documentary filmmaking.

White House Vigils
Conducted 4 CAN-DO vigils in front of the White House. Organized a 5th with #Cut50 and Change.org.
2016
Obama Clemency Wave
Of 105 women who received clemency under Obama, 44 were CAN-DO members that Amy advocated for.
2018
Alice Johnson & First Step Act
CAN-DO recommended Alice Johnson for clemency. Amy championed the First Step Act, landmark bipartisan sentencing reform.
2021
Full Presidential Pardon
On January 20, President Trump granted Amy a full pardon, calling her 'a voice for the incarcerated and a champion for criminal justice reform.'
2024

2025
